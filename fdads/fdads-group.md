# Forever Dads — Group Structure & Facilitation

---

## Purpose & Founding Context

Forever Dads was founded by Kiwifruit in February 2026. It grew from his recognition that adoptive fathers face a specific, under-supported set of challenges — and that there was no peer support group for them.

Founding motivation: Kiwifruit was navigating his own depression and anger dysregulation as an adoptive father and needed a space that didn't yet exist. The group was named and committed to on 25 Feb 2026, shared with the men's circle (The Clearing) the same week.

Kiwifruit's opening statement in Session 1: *"I started this group because I needed it."* This models psychological safety immediately, removes hierarchy, and removes the "expert facilitator" role.

---

## Emerging Themes

The group exists to hold these realities for adoptive fathers — all under-discussed:

- **Depression** — common, often disguised as stress or irritability; naming it is a critical first step
- **Anger dysregulation** — specific to therapeutic parenting demands; not the whole picture of who the dad is
- **Shame** — particularly around anger that feels out of character or that scares the dad himself
- **Identity disruption** — loss of professional self, shift to parent identity, loss of biological parenthood path
- **Financial anxiety** — acute when parenting and job instability coincide
- **Intergenerational trauma** — many adoptive fathers bring unprocessed history into the parenting relationship
- **The performing-fine trap** — high-functioning men presenting as capable while quietly depleting
- **Self-care as medical necessity** — depletion directly undermines therapeutic parenting capacity
- **Toddler projection** — children who expect anger will elicit it because familiarity = safety (Birch insight, Session 2)

## Session Format

### Standard check-in (Sessions 1 + 3 pattern)

Opening round — each person shares:
- Child's name/s, age, time since placement (grounds everyone in shared context, low-stakes)

Three check-in questions (borrowed from The Clearing men's circle):
1. What's bringing you joy this week?
2. What's been weighing on you?
3. What are you looking forward to?

**Order matters:** joy question goes first — lowers the drawbridge before vulnerability is invited. Closing question sends people home oriented forward, not sitting in heaviness.

### PIES check-in (Session 2 format)

Deeper format for smaller groups or when more structure is wanted:
- **P**hysical — body, health, energy
- **I**ntellectual — work, mind, stimulation
- **E**motional — feelings, relationships, inner state
- **S**piritual — meaning, purpose, groundedness

### Process norms (agreed after Session 1)

- Affirmations while someone is speaking are OK (grunts, nods)
- Active feedback or comment is not OK during someone's sharing
- If someone wants to invite feedback: mention it at the end of your sharing (facilitator to include in intro)
- Childcare interruptions welcomed; baby monitors invited
- Sessions will run even if Kiwifruit cannot attend (requires self-facilitation resource — see open actions)

---

## Facilitation Principles

- Go first. Model the honesty you want to see.
- Genuinely receive what others share — don't lead, don't fix.
- The group is not therapy. It's peer support. The distinction matters.
- Connect members to professional support pathways where relevant (Birch has a counsellor referral route).
- Keep the format light enough that the group can self-facilitate without Kiwifruit.

---

## Format Evolution

- Session 1: three check-in questions — worked well; felt calm and relaxed
- Session 2: PIES format — tried with 2-person attendance; gives more structure for smaller numbers
- Session 3+: continue iterating based on attendance and group feedback
