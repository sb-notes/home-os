## NZ Suburb Research Detail (Feb–Mar 2026)

### Why North Shore?

North Shore Auckland was identified as the best fit for the family's criteria over central Auckland, east Auckland, or west Auckland. Reasons:

- Best balance of beach access, walkable village character, and good schools
- More community-oriented than central Auckland CBD suburbs
- Ferry routes to CBD (especially from Devonport, Birkenhead) provide commute options without full car dependency
- Property prices are high but not as extreme as central Auckland
- NZ family connections are in the northwest/north — North Shore gives proximity without being in rural Northland

### Suburb detail notes

**Milford:** The standout. Beach (Milford Beach — sheltered harbour beach, calm water), walkable main street, strong school zone (Westlake Girls, Westlake Boys for secondary), café culture, genuine community feel. Bus routes to Takapuna hub and onward to city. Not a surf beach — more harbour swimming. Hannah's Italian classes: good residential catchment, community hall/library venues likely available.

**Takapuna:** Takapuna Beach is a genuine surf/swim beach (sandy, long). Strong commercial strip — restaurants, cafés, arts. Excellent bus links (Northern Express to CBD is fast, ~25 min). More urban energy. Good choice if Ollie is commuting to Auckland hybrid. Higher property prices than Milford for equivalent space.

**Devonport:** Historic naval village. Victorian and Edwardian architecture. Ferry (20 min to CBD — scenic route, a genuine pleasure). Best cultural/arts scene of the North Shore options. Very tight housing supply — small peninsula. Property is premium. Best fit for Hannah's Italian teaching culturally (artistic, progressive community). Least car-dependent option. Risk: ferry dependency during outages.

**Birkenhead:** Underrated. Central North Shore, bus options, harbour views from higher streets. Local pubs, farmers market, community events. Less gentrified than Takapuna/Milford. Good for stretching budget. Birkenhead Point has a small beach. 

**Titirangi:** West Auckland, Waitakere Ranges, bush/beach (west coast surf beaches nearby — Piha, Karekare). Creative community (art deco village centre, gallery, annual events). Very different lifestyle proposition — more like living in a forest village than a coastal suburb. Car-essential. Long drive to CBD. High appeal to Hannah's arts values. Less practical for Ollie if working hybrid Auckland.

**Waitakere / Te Henga (Bethells Beach):** Remote, beautiful, dramatic black sand surf beach. Rural coastal. Very limited amenities. Monthly cost NZ$7,463–11,108. Only viable as a lifestyle choice if Ollie is fully remote and Hannah is not trying to build a teaching business. Closest to NZ family potentially.

**Matakana:** Wine country, 30 min north of Auckland. Saturday farmers market is exceptional. Boutique village. Very limited housing stock — hard to find anything to buy. Impractical unless they want a rural lifestyle.

**Whangarei:** 90 min north. Northland's main city. Genuinely affordable by NZ standards (NZ$5,370–7,465/month all-in). University, hospital, decent shopping. Less cosmopolitan than Auckland. Relevant if Ollie's NZ family (Sam and family) relocate to nearby area. Would only make sense if the extended family connection is the primary draw.

## Bordeaux Trip — Full Notes (August 2025)

### History and character

- Eleanor of Aquitaine gave Bordeaux to the English crown via her marriage to Henry II in 1152. Remained English for 300 years (hence the English obsession with claret — Bordeaux wine).
- 18th century: Bordeaux became one of France's wealthiest cities through Atlantic trade, including the slave trade from West Africa. The city's beautiful neoclassical architecture was largely funded by this trade — a fact now acknowledged in local memory.
- WWII: Occupied by Nazis from 1940. Complex resistance history. Notable: the SS officer Klaus Barbie (the "Butcher of Lyon") operated in the region.
- Post-war: Progressive transformation. Strong left-wing political tradition. Community urban farming, radical bookshops (like Mollat, France's largest independent bookshop), food market culture (Marché des Capucins), cycling infrastructure.
- Today: UNESCO World Heritage city. Major wine tourism (Cité du Vin is worth visiting despite being slightly corporate). Genuinely vibrant arts scene.

### What they actually did / recommendations

- Bassins de Lumières: excellent immersive art experience in a converted submarine base. Book ahead. Cool temperature inside — good for hot days.
- Basilique Saint-Michel: medieval basilica with separate bell tower. Free organ concerts. Worth it.
- Café Brazza area: across the Garonne on the right bank (la Bastide). Indie café culture, away from tourist circuit. Worth the short tram/bridge walk.
- Place de la Bourse and the mirror pool (Miroir d'Eau): iconic but genuinely beautiful. Best at dawn or dusk when light is soft.
- Marché des Capucins (Marché Victor Hugo): the real market. Not tourist-facing. Go early.
- Botanic Garden (Jardin Botanique du Parc Floral): cooler microclimate, good for prams/toddlers.

### Practical notes

- Heatwave management: stay inside or in shade 11am–5pm. Plan morning and evening outings. Air-conditioned indoor venues (museum, Bassins de Lumières) for midday.
- Nationwide FlexPlus travel insurance: heatwave NOT covered unless government travel ban declared. Climate disruption events are generally excluded from standard travel insurance.
- TBM pass: 7-day pass purchased at tram stop kiosk (physical ticket needed). TBM app for real-time info.
