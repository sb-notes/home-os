  # What Are Schemas?
  Schemas are patterns in children's thinking and play that help us understand what Caspar and Phoenix are passionately exploring. A child can demonstrate one, several, or no schema at a particular time. Schemas help us engage children in sustained, meaningful conversations about their ideas — and to support their exploration by supplying activities that might be relevant, feeding in appropriate language and questions to scaffold their learning most effectively.

  # Application to Phoenix and Caspar (24 months, 1 Mar 2026)
  ## What We're Observing — Schema Mapping
  ### Phoenix
  Phoenix is showing **strong Trajectory schema**: she is vigorously physical, runs everywhere, loves the climbing frame arch and slide, and throws/kicks with purpose and enthusiasm. She's also showing emerging **Rotation and Circularity** — she loves spinning on spot, dancing in circles, and is very drawn to wheeled toys and the rotary motion of things. Her advanced language development and interactive play suggests she is also beginning **Connecting** schema in social terms — she is wiring people together, following leaders, and initiating games like chase and ring-around-the-rosy.
  Her food-related play maps onto **Transforming** — she is fascinated by food preparation, watching things change (mixing, mashing) and the process of cooking. This lines up with her attachment to Hannah around feeding and her hypervigilance around meals.
  For **Enclosure/Enveloping**: her tendency to wrap up comforters, cover herself with blankets and seek enclosed spaces (behind sofa, under table) for emotional regulation is a healthy enclosure schema in action — her nervous system using physical enveloping as a co-regulation tool.
  ### Caspar
  Caspar shows dominant **Transporting schema**: he is a mover of objects — loads things into trucks and prams, carries things from room to room, and is strongly motivated to move heavy or large items. His "train coming at you" quality is pure transporting schema energy — head down, moving from A to B with cargo.
  He also shows clear **Trajectory schema**: he is a basher and a knocker — he loves to knock towers down, throw balls, push cars off edges and watch the arc. His climbing frame use (arch and slide) is entirely trajectory-driven.
  His interest in fitting lids on containers and posting boxes maps to **Enclosure and Enveloping**, and his musical responsiveness to beating rhythms maps onto **Rotation** (the circular motion of percussion, the beat cycle).
  ## What This Tells Us as Parents
  **Schemas are not misbehaviour — they are the curriculum.** When Caspar dumps all the toys out of the box: that is disconnecting and transporting schema. When Phoenix flings food off her tray: that is trajectory schema. When either child wraps themselves or objects in blankets: that is enveloping schema. Understanding this reframes constant correction into constant scaffolding.
  **How to support rather than suppress:**
  - **Transporting (Caspar dominant):** Provide things to load and move — wheelbarrows, prams, baskets, small trolleys. Let him carry the shopping. Give him "purpose-driven" transport tasks. Avoid setting up activities that cannot be transported — provide portability.
  - **Trajectory (both):** Give appropriate trajectory outlets — balls outdoors, ramps, slides, water play with pouring/squirting, pillows to throw. Redirect (not punish) indoor trajectory: "We throw outside, not inside — let's go."
  - **Enclosure/Enveloping (both, especially for regulation):** Provide forts, tunnels, pop-up tents, sleep sacks, weighted blankets. Their use of enclosure is a self-regulation tool — treat it as a resource not a nuisance.
  - **Rotation (Phoenix, Caspar):** Spinning tops, windmills, wheels, mixing play, dancing. Phoenix's dancing is not random — it is rotation schema driven.
  - **Transforming (Phoenix):** Cooking play, playdough, mud kitchen, paint mixing. Feed the transforming schema to reduce food performance anxiety — make transformation safe and fun outside of mealtimes.
  ## Connection to Therapeutic Parenting
  Children who have experienced early adversity often use **schemas as regulation tools** — not just exploration. For Phoenix and Caspar, trajectory and transporting schemas may carry additional meaning: movement = agency, carrying = safety, enclosing = security. Aloyse should be aware that schema-rich environments (especially with outdoor and sensory options) are not just fun — they are the children's primary self-regulation toolkit at this developmental stage.
  ## For Dr Quinn
  This framework is relevant to developmental assessments: both children are showing age-appropriate (or advanced) schema development for 24-month-olds with early adversity backgrounds. Caspar's dominant transporting schema is consistent with a child who found agency and safety through physical action and movement — his primary secure base with foster dad was physically constrained (crutches, couch), so Caspar may have internalised movement as independence. Phoenix's trajectory and rotation schemas align with her hypervigilance — constant scanning, fast movement, and circular reassurance-seeking (come back, check parent, go again). Both are developmentally healthy manifestations.

- **Remove the audience:** One parent engages Caspar (commentary, song, spotting game) during Phoenix's slower eating — not as reward, just as redirect. Takes the show's audience away.

## 🍱 Love Me Feed Me — Feeding Protocol Plan (17 Mar 2026)
### Context
- Both children loudly demand yoghurt at nearly every meal — identified as food-scarcity anxiety from foster care, not behavioural defiance
- Family planning to trial the **Love Me Feed Me** protocol (Dr Katja Rowell) — implementation target approximately late March 2026, once children are more settled into preschool routine
### Protocol structure (breakfast and lunch)
All three courses placed on the table **before the children sit down**:
1. Main course plate
1. 1.5 tbsp stewed apple (per child)
1. 1 tbsp full-fat plain Greek yoghurt in a small dedicated bowl (per child)
- No sequencing enforced — children eat in any order
- No conditional language ("eat your main first", "you can have yoghurt when...") — all food is equally and unconditionally available
- Consistent small bowl per child (same colour/shape each time) reinforces *"this is mine, it is always here"*
### Therapeutic rationale
- Grounded in Ellyn Satter's Division of Responsibility: parents decide **what, when, where**; children decide **whether and how much**
- Visible abundance directly counters the scarcity programme both children are running from early food-insecure experience
- Expected outcome over weeks: loud demanding reduces as nervous system registers consistent food availability and safety
- If a child eats only yoghurt one meal — allow it; this is part of the trust-building process, not a failure
### Timing
- Not implementing immediately — waiting until both children are more settled into preschool transition (~late March 2026)
- Review after 2–4 weeks of consistent implementation
- **Greek yoghurt: ~1 tbsp at lunch and dinner** (not breakfast — see Dr Quinn note on Caspar's breakfast protocol)
- **Illness rule: pause yoghurt for 5 days when either child is sick** (dairy thickens mucus; resume once well)
### Full daily meal structure (retained as-is)
- Breakfast → milk → snack → lunch → juice → snack → dinner → milk
- No changes to existing meal timing or portion sizes
- Routine preservation is a therapeutic priority alongside the feeding approach
### Additional Love Me Feed Me elements
**Fruit bowl — always available**
- A bowl of fruit kept accessible in the kitchen at all times
- Children can see and access fruit freely — reinforces *"food is always here, I don't need to hoard or fight for it"*
- Not a meal replacement; just a visible, permanent abundance signal
**Extended table offering at breakfast, lunch, and dinner**

  ## Food, and the Love Me Feed Me approach
  This is the most important practical section of this document, and the area where we would most value the team's support and partnership.
  In their earlier placement, food was the primary currency of love and reward. The children were overfed at mealtimes, given frequent sweet snacks as performance rewards, and learned to demand food loudly and anxiously as a way of securing attention. When they came to us, both showed strong food-scarcity behaviours — rapid eating, hoarding, distress when a meal was delayed by even a few minutes, and a generalised belief that food might not come again.
  We are now following a therapeutic feeding approach called Love Me Feed Me, developed by the paediatric dietician Dr Katja Rowell specifically for children from care backgrounds. The core principle is simple: the child needs to learn, over many repeated experiences, that food will always come, that there is always enough of it, and that they will never be told they can't have more of the good stuff. Once their nervous system registers this reliably, the anxiety around food begins to melt — and we are already seeing that happen at home.
  In practice this means that for this first active phase of the protocol we are letting them eat as much as they want of healthy food at each meal. What you may notice in practice is that they are currently eating very large breakfasts — roughly twice what a two-year-old would normally eat — which sometimes means they come to preschool less interested in morning snack than other children, and may then eat a smaller lunch. This is entirely expected and is not a sign that anything is wrong. The pattern of a very large meal followed by a small one is part of how their appetite is beginning to self-regulate.
  The single most helpful thing the preschool team can do to support this work is to avoid using conditional language around food. Phrases like "you can have X when you've finished Y", "only if you eat your main first", or "that's enough now" — which are completely normal and appropriate with most children — can reactivate the scarcity alarm for Caspar and Phoenix. Instead, offering food simply and without conditions, letting them decide how much they eat, and being matter-of-fact if they don't want something, is exactly what we're doing at home and will reinforce the same message. If either of them appears distressed at the end of a meal or snack time, a warm calm presence and a reassurance along the lines of "there will be more later" is the most helpful response we've found.
  On arrival at preschool Phoenix in particular sometimes shows a strong food-security reflex — wanting something to eat or to hold in her hand almost immediately. If this comes up, giving her something small straight away rather than asking her to wait is the approach that works for us at home and consistently defuses the anxiety.
