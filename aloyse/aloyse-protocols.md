# Aloyse — Parenting Protocols & Frameworks

---

## Love Me Feed Me (LMFM) Protocol

Based on Dr Katja Rowell's framework, grounded in Ellyn Satter's Division of Responsibility: parents decide what, when, where; children decide whether and how much. Visible abundance directly counters the food-scarcity programme both children carry from early experience.

### Protocol structure — full implementation (active from 20 March 2026)

All three courses placed on the table before children sit down at every meal:
1. Main course
2. 1.5 tbsp stewed apple with chia seeds (per child)
3. 1 tbsp full-fat plain live-culture Greek yoghurt in a small dedicated bowl (per child, same colour/shape each time)

Additional small bowls of freely available top-up foods also on table: extra cooked vegetables, extra carbohydrates (pasta, rice, bread, oats). Fruit bowl always accessible in kitchen.

No sequencing enforced. No conditional language ("eat your main first", "you can have yoghurt when…"). Children eat in any order. If a child eats only yoghurt — allow it; this is part of trust-building.

Yoghurt illness rule: pause for 5 days when either child is sick (dairy thickens mucus); resume once well.

### Meal-ending strategies (in use / under refinement)

- **Chippy transition** (Carciofi, confirmed working): single potato chip offered only when children are clearly full but still attending to food. Both children respond immediately — get down, have chip, return to play. Works even with grandparents on video call as distraction.
- **Milk transition at breakfast**: "Oh Fox is going to have milk. Does Bunny want milk?" — works well to close breakfast.
- **Pudding protocol** (trialled 13 Apr, positive): clear food from table before apple/yoghurt offered as pudding; offered once children begin refusing main. Reduces open-ended grazing.
- **Ham rule**: remove from table once initial serving given — otherwise Bunny continues reaching.
- Outstanding: children still cry bitterly when food ends. Nerve required — protocol is working even though endings remain hard.

### Trajectory (by April 2026)

Week 1 (late March): 3–4x normal portions, meal duration 1–1.5 hrs each, enormous poos from both. Week 2: portions beginning to reduce; novelty wearing off. By mid-April (day ~30): portions approaching 1.25–1.5x normal at most meals; food anxiety measurably lower; no between-meal demands; children regulated enough to say "tato please" and do thank-you motion.

LMFM-predicted boundary explosion confirmed: as food-scarcity stress reduced, both children testing all other edges with "newfound rigour." Positive prognostic — relief from core fear makes more room to test others.

Social eating context: Fox's older urgency pattern reactivates in group settings (birthday parties, other children eating). Not regression — context-specific activation. Continue monitoring.

### Overnight oats protocol

Plain base for children: oats, water, cinnamon, few sultanas. Not the full adult version. Fox: half bran flakes / half overnight oats. Bunny: half rice bubbles + cornflakes / half overnight oats (lower fibre to manage stool consistency).

## Theraplay Framework

**Structure:** Hold the shape of the day even when Fox or Bunny is chaotic — calm persistence, not winning the battle. Routine is the regulatory scaffold; disruptions of 60–90 minutes carry real dysregulation cost for these two specifically.

**Nurture:** After every dysregulation, a deliberate micro-moment of warmth — hand on back, "you alright mate", offer food or drink. This is the repair.

**Check-in rhythm:** When Fox is calm and independent, brief check-in: "There he is. I see you." Regular micro-deposits of attunement reduce the need to break things to get noticed.

**Engagement:** Turn-taking games with clear structure; Kiwifruit or Carciofi in charge of start, pace, and end. Pre-empt escalation by giving Fox a role rather than managing behaviour.

## Therapeutic Parenting Principles

### Toddler projection mechanism (Josh, Forever Dads)
Toddlers project emotions from their past. If they expect a carer to be angry (because that was their experience), they will find ways to elicit that anger because familiarity feels safe and predictable. Fox in particular — ambivalent attachment testing may partially be driven by trying to recreate a familiar emotional map. Therapeutic response: do not confirm that map. Calm, warm, regulated re-entry breaks the cycle.

### Attachment signals and distress response
For children with early relational trauma, crying is an attachment communication, not manipulation. Once children cross from independent play into distress, the attachment system is activated. The independent-play phase of morning wait is developmentally fine; audible distress is the signal to act.

### Routine as regulatory scaffold
Both children's nervous systems are tightly coupled to routine. Routine disruptions of even 60–90 minutes produce significant dysregulation cost — confirmed repeatedly (Brighton day trip, family visit, Easter break). This is clinical necessity for children from care simultaneously navigating daycare transition, not pedantry.

### Parental regulation is the foundation
Kiwifruit's anger regulation work directly benefits Fox (primary target of dysregulated responses). Key mechanism: "observer self" — watching the feeling from outside creates a gap for micro-pause and breath. Anchor word: Gina. Key insight: "It's no longer just my fault, but the solution is my responsibility." As Kiwifruit builds regulation, Fox's testing behaviours are expected to reduce.

Kiwifruit's overachiever's parenting cycle: exceptional parent most of the time → cracks from no self-care → guilt → repeat. Resolution: self-care (float, yoga, walks, men's circle) is parenting infrastructure, not earned reward.

### Sibling dynamics
Bunny is currently the aggressor in sibling conflict — high-functioning, anxious, vigilant; becomes aggressive when dysregulated from overtiredness or routine disruption. Fox is gentle and not yet aware he is bigger. Future watchpoint: dynamic may shift when Fox realises his size advantage. Fox's physicality (pushing Bunny with body weight) is a new pattern emerging April 2026 — redirect, don't suppress.

### Bedtime ritual
Both children now touch the light and the breathing bear as part of bedtime ritual. Allow these. Toniebox in use: Bedtime Lullabies for wind-down; Nature Sounds or Sleepy Sheep overnight.

## Snack and Feeding Dynamics

### Bunny lording snack over Fox
**What works:** "Eat it or daddy puts it in the bin" — neutral, drama-free, removes Fox from the equation.
**What backfires:** "Finish it or daddy takes it and no more snack" — threat with punishment; reinforces cry-to-reverse loop. Retired.
**Remove the audience:** One parent engages Fox during Bunny's slower eating — commentary, song, spotting game. Takes the show's audience away.
**Parallel narration, not reaction:** When Bunny waves snack, ignore behaviour; narrate warmly to Fox: "You've had yours, well done mate."
**Theraplay moment for Fox at handout:** Eye contact, deliberate handover — "Here's yours, Cas." Installs "food comes from calm, reliable source."

### Do not put children in direct competition for snack under any circumstances.

### Vegetable transfer (Bunny → Fox)
"Shall we give your veg to Caspar?" — Bunny chooses to eat rather than yield. Removes power struggle. Fox benefits from extra veg. Fits LMFM autonomy framework. Retained.
